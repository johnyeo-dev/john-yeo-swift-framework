Community Building · Extra Activities

🎉 Parties for Kids — Build Community With Your Students

These are extra activities you can run alongside your teaching. They're not compulsory — but done right, they turn students and parents into a loyal community that stays and refers.

🪜

The 8 Steps

1

Ask the Kids What They Want

Don't plan in a vacuum. Let students tell you what they actually want.

"What do you want to eat? What do you want to do?"

CC's kids gave her their menu. She cooked or ordered accordingly. That's it.

2

Keep It Low-Key and Low-Pressure

You don't need a programme or itinerary. Food + free play is enough. Kids organise themselves once they're comfortable with each other.

3

Small Groups, Not One Big Event

Cap at 20 per session. If you have more students, run multiple smaller sessions — by level, by cohort, or by interest.

4

Match Activities to What You Enjoy Too

CC offered what she was genuinely interested in. Don't force what drains you.

  • Art jamming / free-flow painting
  • Baking (small group)
  • Sports / football outing
  • Eating together at home
5

End-of-Year Incentive Trip for Graduating Cohort

CC sponsors a USS trip for all P6 students at year-end. Rules are simple:

  • Only P6s get sponsored entry
  • Siblings and parents can come but pay their own way
  • Kids roam freely — she's available if they need help

It becomes something students look forward to from P5. They ask about it. They work for it.

6

Don't Announce It as a Sales Pitch

She doesn't put the trip in her marketing. Word spreads through students and Instagram. The mystique is part of the appeal.

7

Invite Parents but Let Them Self-Organise Too

Some parents volunteer to come. She places extrovert parents between quieter ones to get conversation going. Parents end up bonding with each other — and she becomes the reason their community exists.

8

Do It Because You Mean It, Not Because It's a Strategy

The moment parents sense it's transactional, it loses its power.

"All parents need their community too. As a teacher, we are just a catalyst to build community for them." — CC
💡 Start small: One casual meal with your current students this term. Let them pick the food. That's your first community event.
