Just in Time, Not Just in Case

One of the biggest silent killers in building a scalable coaching or tutoring business isn't laziness. It's the opposite — over-preparing for problems that haven't arrived yet, and hoarding knowledge you don't need right now.

"I'm going to be honest with you — I still struggle with this. I find myself chasing resources, saving courses, buying tools, researching ideas... just in case I need them one day. My wife once told me: focus on the next two hours. That simple line changed how I work. This page exists because I needed it too."

— John, founder of SWIFT Framework
The Two Modes

Most of us default to one of these without realising it. Understanding the difference is the first step to changing your pattern.

The Trap

Just in Case Thinking

  • Collecting resources before you need them
  • Learning everything before starting
  • Waiting until you feel "ready"
  • Preparing for every possible scenario
  • Buying tools you're not yet using
  • Saying yes to things that might be useful

The Shift

Just in Time Thinking

  • Learning what you need, when you need it
  • Starting before you feel fully ready
  • Solving problems as they actually appear
  • Saying no to what isn't relevant right now
  • Taking the next step, not ten steps ahead
  • Trusting the process to teach you as you go
Why Tutors Are Especially Vulnerable

As educators, we were trained to know before we teach. That instinct is a strength in the classroom — but it becomes a trap when you're building a business. You keep waiting to know enough before you start. The truth is: you already know more than your next student needs.

How to Shift — Step by Step

These aren't quick fixes. They are habits to practise, especially on the days when anxiety kicks in and the urge to "prepare more" gets loud.

1

Name what you're actually afraid of

Just in case thinking is usually anxiety wearing a productivity costume. Before you open another tab or buy another course, ask yourself what you're really trying to avoid. Failure? Embarrassment? Being asked a question you can't answer?

Ask: "What am I afraid will happen if I don't prepare for this right now?"
2

Define your next 2 hours, not your next 2 months

The moment your planning horizon stretches past what you can act on today, you're in just in case territory. Bring it back. What is the one thing that moves your work forward in the next 2 hours?

Ask: "What is the most useful thing I can do right now — not eventually?"
3

Learn in response to real problems, not imagined ones

You don't need a full Facebook ads course before you run your first ad. Run the ad. When a real problem appears — low click-through, wrong audience — go learn exactly that one thing. The problem becomes your curriculum.

Ask: "Has this problem actually happened, or am I solving a ghost?"
4

Create a "parking lot" for good ideas that aren't for now

Not everything you shelve is wasted. Keep a simple notes doc called "later" — for tools, ideas, courses, strategies that feel important but aren't urgent. This lets your brain let go without losing the idea. Revisit it monthly.

Ask: "Is this for now or for later?" Then park it and move on.
5

Build trust with yourself by finishing small things

Just in case thinking often comes from a lack of trust — you don't believe you can handle what comes next, so you try to pre-load everything. The cure is evidence. Finish small things. Notice that you figured it out. Build the track record that tells your brain: "I can handle this when it comes."

Ask: "What is one small thing I can finish today to build that trust?"
6

Protect your energy like it's your most valuable resource

Because it is. Every time you chase something just in case, you're spending attention you could have given to the student in front of you, the post you haven't written, or the rest you actually need. Energy is the foundation of everything else in your business.

Ask: "Would I still do this if I only had 3 hours of good energy today?"
Quick Action Steps to Start Today

Pick one. Just one. That's the point.

Today

Write down the one thing that actually needs to happen this week. Cross off everything that is "nice to have."

This Week

Create a "later" note or folder. Move 3 things from your current to-do list into it. Notice how that feels.

Ongoing Habit

Before starting any new learning or research, ask: "Does a real problem exist right now that requires this?"

With Your Coach

In your next session, bring 3 things you've been "preparing" for. We'll decide together which ones are real and which are just fear wearing a busy hat.

The SWIFT Framework is built on the idea that you don't need more information to move forward. You need the right information at the right time, paired with the courage to act before you feel fully ready.

Just in time thinking isn't careless. It's intentional trust — in yourself, in your clients, and in the process of learning by doing.

The tutors who build sustainable, peaceful businesses aren't the ones who prepared the most. They're the ones who started, stayed present, and solved problems as they came.
